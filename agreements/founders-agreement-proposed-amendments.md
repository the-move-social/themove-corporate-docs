## 1. Reverse Vesting

**Current State:** Section 2.2 says each Founder's shares are "fully vested and non-forfeitable as of the date of issuance." There is no vesting schedule.

**Proposed Change:** Amend Section 2.2 and add a new Section 2.3 introducing a 4-year reverse vesting schedule with a 1-year cliff.

**How It Works:**

- Each Founder's 4,250,000 shares become subject to the Company's right to repurchase unvested shares at the original purchase price ($0.00001 per share — essentially free) if that Founder leaves.
- **Year 1 (the "cliff"):** Zero shares vest during the first 12 months. On the 1-year anniversary, 25% (1,062,500 shares) vest all at once.
- **Months 13–48:** The remaining 75% vest monthly in equal installments (roughly 88,541 shares per month) over the next 36 months.
- After 4 full years, all shares are fully vested and the repurchase right expires.

**Acceleration events (shares vest immediately in full):**

- A Change of Control occurs (the company is acquired). This is called "single-trigger acceleration" — it protects both founders from losing unvested equity in an exit.
- A Founder is removed without Cause. If you're fired unfairly, you shouldn't lose your equity.

**Why This Matters:** This is the single most important gap in the current agreement. Without vesting, either founder could leave on day two and walk away with 50% of the company. Reverse vesting is the universal standard for startup founders — investors will almost certainly require it anyway, and having it already in place signals sophistication. It protects both founders equally: if one person does all the work for 3 years while the other leaves after 2 months, the departing founder doesn't keep half the company.

**Impact on Departure (Section 9.3):** When a founder voluntarily departs, any unvested shares are repurchased by the Company at par value. Only vested shares follow the existing conversion/retention mechanics (keep as Class A, convert to Class C, or sell at FMV).

## 2. Right of First Refusal

**Current State:** The agreement has no general restriction on transferring shares to third parties. The shotgun mechanism (Section 4) handles founder-to-founder buyouts, but there's nothing preventing a founder from selling shares to an outsider.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 2.5 — Right of First Refusal (ROFR).

**How It Works:**

- Before either Founder can transfer Class A shares to any third party, they must first offer those shares to the other Founder and the Company at the same price and on the same terms.
- The other Founder and the Company have 30 days to exercise the ROFR.
- If neither exercises, the selling Founder may proceed with the third-party sale — but only at the price and terms originally offered.
- The ROFR does not apply to transfers under the shotgun mechanism (Section 4), which has its own rules.

**Why This Matters:** Without a ROFR, one founder could sell their 50% stake to a stranger — a competitor, a hostile actor, or someone neither founder wants involved. The ROFR ensures each founder has a chance to keep the cap table clean and avoid unwanted third-party stockholders. This is standard in virtually every startup stockholders' agreement and will be expected by any future investor.

## 3. Anti-Dilution / Preemptive Rights

**Current State:** No provision protects either founder from dilution in a future equity issuance.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 2.6 — Anti-Dilution Protection (Preemptive Rights).

**How It Works:**

- In any future issuance of equity (new shares, convertible notes, SAFEs, etc.), each Founder has the right to purchase their pro rata share of the new securities on the same terms offered to other investors.
- **Example:** If the Company issues 1,000,000 new shares to an investor, and you currently own 50% of the company, you have the right to purchase up to 500,000 of those new shares at the same price, to maintain your 50% ownership.
- This right does not apply to shares issued under the Equity Incentive Plan (the Class B option pool), which is already carved out and expected to dilute both founders equally.
- The right is exercisable within 15 days of receiving notice of the proposed issuance.

**Why This Matters:** Without preemptive rights, one founder (acting with Board approval under a future expanded Board) could authorize an equity issuance that dilutes the other founder's ownership without giving them a chance to participate. Preemptive rights ensure each founder can maintain their stake if they choose to invest alongside outside investors.

## 4. Drag-Along Rights

**Current State:** No drag-along provision exists.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 2.7 — Drag-Along Rights.

**How It Works:**

- If holders of more than 50% of the outstanding Class A Common Stock approve a Change of Control transaction (sale of the company, merger, etc.), all other stockholders are required to participate in the transaction on the same terms.
- The dragged stockholders receive the same price per share and the same protections as the majority holders.
- This right is mutual — today it applies equally to both founders (since each holds exactly 50%, it would require both to agree anyway). It becomes critical later if there are additional stockholders (investors, employees who exercised options, etc.).

**Why This Matters:** Drag-along rights prevent a minority stockholder from blocking a sale that the majority wants to proceed with. While this isn't an issue today (both founders hold 50% and all Board decisions require unanimity), it becomes essential once there are investors or other stockholders. Acquirers will insist on being able to buy 100% of the company — if a minority holder can refuse, it kills deals. Having this in place signals investor readiness.

## 5. Tag-Along Rights (General)

**Current State:** The agreement has a limited tag-along right in Section 4.6(f), but it only applies to Class C shares in the context of a shotgun buyout.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 2.8 — General Tag-Along Rights.

**How It Works:**

- If any stockholder receives a bona fide third-party offer to purchase more than 50% of their shares, all other stockholders have the right to sell a pro rata portion of their shares in the same transaction, on the same terms and at the same price per share.
- The selling stockholder must notify all other stockholders of the offer and give them 15 days to elect to participate.
- If the buyer refuses to purchase the additional shares, the original seller may not proceed with the sale.

**Why This Matters:** Tag-along rights protect minority stockholders from being left behind in a transaction. If one founder negotiates a premium sale of their shares, the other founder should have the option to participate rather than being stuck holding shares in a company now controlled by someone else. This is the mirror image of drag-along rights — drag-along prevents blocking; tag-along prevents exclusion.

## 6. Compensation Terms

**Current State:** The agreement defines roles (Section 3) and time commitment (Section 3.4) but never addresses how founders get paid. Section 3.4 references "compensation (whether salary, consulting fees, or other regular payment)" without defining how it's set.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 3.6 — Compensation.

**How It Works:**

- All founder compensation (salary, consulting fees, bonuses, benefits, or any other form of payment) must be approved by unanimous Board resolution.
- Neither Founder may receive any compensation from the Company without the other Founder's explicit written consent.
- Compensation decisions shall be revisited at least annually or whenever the Company's financial position materially changes.
- This section cross-references the existing independent contractor agreements already in the `/agreements/` directory.

**Why This Matters:** Compensation is one of the most common sources of founder disputes. Without clear rules, one founder could theoretically propose an outsized salary, and the other might feel pressured to agree. By requiring unanimous Board approval (which already implicitly exists under Section 15.7's consensus voting), this section makes the rule explicit and removes ambiguity. It also creates a paper trail that investors will expect to see.

## 7. Founder Loans

**Current State:** No provision addresses what happens when a founder lends money to the company or pays company expenses out of pocket beyond the $250 reimbursement threshold.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 12.6 — Founder Loans.

**How It Works:**

- Any loan, advance, or out-of-pocket expenditure by a Founder on behalf of the Company exceeding $250 must be:
  - Documented in a written promissory note
  - Approved by both Founders
  - At a commercially reasonable interest rate (e.g., the applicable federal rate, or AFR)
- Founder loans are repaid before any dividends or distributions to stockholders (unless both Founders agree otherwise).
- This prevents one founder from accumulating undocumented IOUs and then demanding repayment at an inconvenient time.

**Why This Matters:** In early-stage companies, founders frequently pay for things out of pocket — a domain name here, a software subscription there. Without rules, these informal loans can pile up, creating disagreements about who's owed what. Documenting them properly also matters for tax purposes and investor due diligence. It's a small provision that prevents a common source of resentment.

## 8. Advisory Tiebreaker for Deadlocks

**Current State:** Section 7 provides a dispute resolution ladder: good faith negotiation (15 days) → mediation (30 days) → binding arbitration. Section 4 provides the shotgun mechanism as a last resort. There's no middle ground between "let's talk about it" and "one of us buys the other out."

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 7.4 — Advisory Tiebreaker.

**How It Works:**

- When the Board reaches a deadlock on any matter, either Founder may request the appointment of an independent advisory director.
- The advisory director is mutually agreed upon. If the Founders can't agree, each selects one candidate, and those two candidates jointly select a third person, who serves as the advisor. (This mirrors the FMV appraiser selection process in Section 1.3.)
- The advisory director reviews the deadlocked matter, hears both sides, and issues a non-binding recommendation within 15 days.
- Both Founders must consider the recommendation in good faith. They are not required to follow it.
- If the deadlock persists after the advisory recommendation, the existing mediation → arbitration → shotgun escalation path remains available.

**Why This Matters:** The current agreement jumps from "talk it out" to "mediation with a professional mediator" to "the nuclear option of the shotgun." Many real-world deadlocks are judgment calls — should we hire this person, should we pivot the product, should we spend money on this marketing campaign. These don't warrant mediation or a forced buyout. An advisory tiebreaker gives both founders a credible outside perspective that often breaks the logjam without escalation. It's cheaper, faster, and less adversarial than mediation.

## 9. Cooling-Off Period

**Current State:** When a deadlock is declared and mediation is initiated, there's no protection against either founder taking unilateral action on the deadlocked issue during the transition.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 7.5 — Cooling-Off Period.

**How It Works:**

- When either Founder provides written notice of a deadlock, a mandatory 10-day cooling-off period begins immediately.
- During this period, neither Founder may take any unilateral action on the deadlocked matter.
- The cooling-off period runs concurrently with (not in addition to) the 15-day negotiation period in Section 7.1.
- After the cooling-off period, the normal dispute resolution ladder continues.

**Why This Matters:** Deadlocks are often heated. The cooling-off period prevents either founder from making a rash decision — signing a contract, firing a contractor, canceling a partnership — while emotions are running high. It's a small procedural guardrail that costs nothing but can prevent irreversible damage.

## 10. Spending Threshold Adjustment Mechanism

**Current State:** Section 12 sets static thresholds: $500 for dual authorization, $2,000 monthly aggregate cap, $250 for expense reimbursements. These numbers are appropriate for a pre-revenue startup but will become absurdly low as the company grows.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 12.6 — Threshold Adjustment.

**How It Works:**

- The spending thresholds in Sections 12.1–12.4 must be reviewed at least once per calendar year by unanimous Board resolution.
- In addition, a mandatory review is triggered by any of the following events:
  - (a) The Company raises external financing (any amount)
  - (b) The Company's monthly recurring revenue exceeds $10,000
  - (c) Either Founder requests a review
- Adjusted thresholds take effect upon Board resolution and are documented in the Company's corporate records.
- If the Founders cannot agree on new thresholds, the existing thresholds remain in place until the disagreement is resolved through the dispute resolution process.

**Why This Matters:** If the company raises a $2M seed round, a $500 dual-signature requirement will paralyze day-to-day operations. But removing spending controls entirely would eliminate a key protection. The adjustment mechanism lets the controls grow with the company while keeping both founders in the loop. The triggering events ensure the review happens at the moments when thresholds are most likely to become outdated.

## 11. Annual Operating Budget

**Current State:** No budget process is defined. Spending is controlled only by the per-transaction and monthly thresholds in Section 12.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 12.7 — Approved Budget.

**How It Works:**

- Both Founders must adopt an annual operating budget by unanimous Board resolution no later than January 31 of each calendar year.
- Expenditures that fall within the approved budget may be made by either Founder individually without additional approval, subject to the existing dual-authorization thresholds.
- Any expenditure that falls outside the approved budget — even if it's below the dual-authorization threshold — requires both Founders' written approval.
- The budget should cover major categories: technology infrastructure, marketing, legal, professional services, contractor compensation, and miscellaneous operations.

**Why This Matters:** A budget creates a shared financial plan. Instead of approving every $600 expense one at a time, both founders agree at the start of the year on where money should go. This speeds up operations (no need to chase dual authorization for budgeted items) while maintaining control over unplanned spending. Investors will also expect to see a budget process in place.

## 12. Credit and Debt Restrictions

**Current State:** Section 12 covers expenditures but says nothing about borrowing money. A founder could theoretically take out a credit card or line of credit in the company's name.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 12.8 — Credit and Debt.

**How It Works:**

- The Company may not incur any indebtedness — including loans, credit lines, corporate credit cards, guarantees, or any other obligation to repay borrowed money — without the unanimous written approval of both Founders.
- This applies regardless of the amount. Even a $100 credit line requires both founders' consent.
- Personal guarantees by either Founder on behalf of the Company require both Founders' written consent.

**Why This Matters:** Debt creates obligations that can threaten the company's survival. A single founder taking on debt unilaterally could expose both founders (and their personal assets, in the case of personal guarantees) to risk they never agreed to. This provision is a straightforward extension of the dual-authorization principle already embedded in Section 12.

## 13. Extended Confidentiality Period

**Current State:** Section 13.3(b) provides a 3-year post-termination confidentiality period for non-trade-secret Confidential Information (trade secrets are protected indefinitely).

**Proposed Change:** Extend Section 13.3(b) from 3 years to 5 years.

**How It Works:**

- After a Founder's departure, the obligation to keep non-trade-secret Confidential Information confidential lasts for 5 years instead of 3.
- Trade secrets remain protected indefinitely (no change).

**Why This Matters:** Three years is on the shorter end for technology companies. Business plans, financial data, partnership terms, and marketing strategies remain competitively sensitive for longer than 3 years in most cases. Five years is the more common standard and aligns with the pace at which early-stage startup information actually becomes stale. The change is small but eliminates a gap that a sophisticated counterparty (or investor) would flag.

## 14. Pre-Existing IP Schedule

**Current State:** Section 14.2 says pre-existing IP "shall be governed by a separate IP Assignment Agreement." That separate agreement exists (`ip-assignment-agreement.md`) and assigns IP to Present Tense Labs, which then licenses it to TMS via `ip-license-agreement.md`. But the founders' agreement itself doesn't enumerate what IP each founder brought in.

**Proposed Change:** Amend Section 14.2 and attach Exhibit B.

**How It Works:**

- Section 14.2 is updated to cross-reference both the IP Assignment Agreement (assigning to PTL) and the IP License Agreement (licensing to TMS).
- A new Exhibit B is attached listing each Founder's pre-existing IP that has been contributed to or is being used by the Company.
- Each Founder has 30 days from execution to complete their section of Exhibit B.
- Any pre-existing IP not listed on Exhibit B is presumed to not have been contributed to the Company.

**Why This Matters:** Without a clear list, disputes can arise about whether a particular piece of technology or content existed before the company or was created for it. If it existed before, it may belong to the founder personally (or to PTL). If it was created for TMS, it belongs to TMS under Section 14.1. The schedule draws a bright line. It also matters for investor due diligence — VCs will want to see a clean IP chain of title.

## 15. Moral Rights Waiver

**Current State:** Section 14.1 assigns all Work Product to the Company, but doesn't address moral rights.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 14.4 — Moral Rights Waiver.

**How It Works:**

- To the extent permitted by applicable law, each Founder waives all "moral rights" in any Work Product assigned to the Company.
- Moral rights typically include: the right to be credited as the author, the right to prevent modification of the work, and the right to prevent uses of the work that could harm the author's reputation.
- This waiver does not affect ownership rights (which are already assigned under Section 14.1) — it addresses the narrower category of rights that some jurisdictions grant to creators even after they've assigned ownership.

**Why This Matters:** In some jurisdictions (particularly outside the US, but also in some US state laws), creators retain certain "moral rights" even after assigning copyright. If a founder later objects to how the company modifies or uses software they wrote, moral rights could theoretically create friction. The waiver eliminates this. It's a standard clause in IP assignment provisions and is often requested during investor due diligence.

## 16. Non-Compete Jurisdictional Carve-Out

**Current State:** Section 5 imposes a 12-month, US-wide non-compete. Section 5.4 states that "each Founder acknowledges that the restrictions are reasonable." But California and several other states effectively ban non-competes for employees, and some are extending those bans to contractors and equity holders.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 5.5 — Jurisdictional Carve-Out.

**How It Works:**

- If any court or jurisdiction finds the non-compete unenforceable (in whole or in part), the restriction is automatically reformed to the maximum scope that the jurisdiction will enforce.
- In jurisdictions that prohibit non-competes entirely (e.g., California), the non-compete is severed, but the non-solicitation (Section 6) and confidentiality (Section 13) provisions remain in full force as independent covenants.
- This is a "savings clause" — it saves the rest of the agreement from being dragged down by one unenforceable provision.

**Why This Matters:** Without this clause, a court in California could void the non-compete and potentially cast doubt on the enforceability of related provisions (non-solicitation, confidentiality) if they're seen as intertwined. The carve-out makes clear that each restrictive covenant stands on its own. It also prevents a departing founder from arguing that the entire restrictive covenant package is void because one piece is unenforceable in their state.

## 17. Garden Leave Payments

**Current State:** The non-compete restricts a departing founder from working in IRL social/adventure apps or social event platforms for 12 months, but the company provides no compensation during that restriction period.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 5.6 — Garden Leave.

**How It Works:**

- During the 12-month non-compete period, the Company pays the departing Founder 50% of their most recent monthly compensation (salary or consulting fee) each month.
- Payment is conditioned on the departing Founder's continued compliance with the non-compete. If they violate it, payments stop.
- The percentage (50%) is a starting point — this should be discussed and agreed upon by both founders.

**Why This Matters:** Several states (including Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington) now require that non-compete agreements include "garden leave" — continued compensation during the restricted period — or the non-compete is void. Even in states that don't require it, providing consideration for the non-compete dramatically increases enforceability. A court is much more likely to enforce a restriction on someone's livelihood if the company is paying them during that period. It also creates a mutual incentive: the departing founder has a financial reason to comply, and the company has a financial reason not to enforce frivolously.

## 18. Future Financing Provisions

**Current State:** The agreement has no provisions addressing what happens when the company raises outside investment. No framework for board expansion, preferred stock, or investor information rights.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 16 — Future Financing Provisions (five subsections).

### 16.1 — Preemptive Rights
Cross-references the anti-dilution provision in Section 2.6. Ensures each Founder can maintain their ownership percentage in future rounds.

### 16.2 — Board Expansion
- In connection with a "qualified financing" (defined as raising $500,000 or more in a single round or series of related closings), the Board may be expanded to include one investor-designated director.
- Both Founders must approve the specific individual proposed for the investor seat.
- The Board size reverts if the investor's ownership falls below a threshold to be negotiated in the investment documents.

**Why this subsection matters:** Investors in a priced round will almost always require a board seat. Having a framework already in place — including the founders' right to approve the specific person — prevents the negotiation from starting from zero and gives the founders leverage to maintain control of the Board composition.

### 16.3 — Preferred Stock Authorization
- Both Founders commit to cooperating in amending the Certificate of Incorporation to authorize a new class of Preferred Stock when a qualified financing is imminent.
- The Preferred Stock will carry customary investor protections: liquidation preference, anti-dilution adjustment, protective provisions (veto rights on major decisions), and conversion rights.
- Neither Founder may unreasonably withhold consent to the amendment.

**Why this subsection matters:** The current Certificate of Incorporation only authorizes Class A and Class B Common Stock. Investors will require Preferred Stock with special rights. Without this cooperation commitment, one founder could theoretically block the amendment and kill the deal. This provision removes that risk while still requiring that the terms be reasonable.

### 16.4 — Information Rights
- The Company will provide investors with customary information rights as negotiated in the applicable investment documents (typically: annual audited financials, quarterly unaudited financials, monthly budget-to-actual reports, and annual operating plan).
- This is a commitment in principle — the specific terms will be negotiated with investors.

### 16.5 — Drag-Along
Cross-references Section 2.7. Ensures the drag-along right extends to any future financing or exit transaction.

**Why Section 16 Matters Overall:** The current agreement is built for a two-founder company with no outside investors. That's appropriate for today, but raising a seed or Series A will require significant modifications to the corporate structure. By pre-negotiating these frameworks between the founders now — while their interests are fully aligned — they avoid having to negotiate under the pressure of an active term sheet. Investors also view pre-existing frameworks as a sign of sophistication and seriousness.

## 19. Blank-Check Preferred Stock Authorization

**Current State:** The Certificate of Incorporation authorizes only two classes of stock: Class A Common (voting) and Class B Common (non-voting, for the option pool).

**Proposed Change:** Amend the Certificate of Incorporation to add a new Article authorizing blank-check Preferred Stock.

**How It Works:**

- A new article authorizes up to a specified number of shares of Preferred Stock (e.g., 5,000,000 shares), par value $0.00001 per share.
- The Board of Directors has authority to issue Preferred Stock in one or more series, and to fix the rights, preferences, and limitations of each series (including: dividend rights, liquidation preference, conversion rights, voting rights, and redemption rights).
- No Preferred Stock is actually issued — this just creates the authorization so it's available when needed.

**Why This Matters:** This is table-stakes for investor readiness. Without Preferred Stock authorization in the Certificate, the company cannot issue Series Seed or Series A shares without first amending the Certificate — which requires a stockholder vote, a Delaware filing, and processing time. Having the blank-check authorization in place means the company can move quickly when a term sheet arrives. Every VC-backed Delaware C-Corp has this provision.

**Note:** This is a separate document (amendment to the Certificate of Incorporation), not a change to the Founders' Agreement itself. The Founders' Agreement in Section 16.3 commits both founders to cooperating on this filing.

## 20. Incapacity Proxy Designation Form (Exhibit A)

**Current State:** Section 1.6(d) requires each Founder to designate an incapacity proxy and an independent tiebreaker within 30 days of signing. But there's no form or template for this designation.

**Proposed Change:** Attach Exhibit A — Incapacity Proxy Designation Form.

**How It Works:**

The form includes:
- The Founder's name
- The designated incapacity proxy (name, contact information, relationship to Founder)
- The designated independent tiebreaker (name, contact information, qualifications)
- A brief description of the proxy's authority (mirroring the limits in Section 1.6(a))
- Signature and date lines
- Instructions for updating the designation

Each Founder completes their own form and delivers it to the other Founder and to the Company's corporate records within 30 days of executing the Agreement.

**Why This Matters:** Section 1.6(d) is one of the most practically important provisions in the agreement — it determines who acts on behalf of an incapacitated founder. But without a form, the requirement is easy to forget or complete inconsistently. The exhibit ensures both founders complete the designation in a uniform format that can be easily located in the corporate records when it's actually needed (which, by definition, will be during a crisis).

## 21. Pre-Existing IP Schedule Form (Exhibit B)

**Current State:** Section 14.2 references a separate IP Assignment Agreement but doesn't identify what IP each founder brought into the company.

**Proposed Change:** Attach Exhibit B — Pre-Existing IP Schedule.

**How It Works:**

The schedule is a table with the following columns for each founder:
- Description of the intellectual property
- Type (software, design, content, patent, trademark, etc.)
- Current ownership (Founder personally, PTL, other)
- Status (assigned to PTL, licensed to TMS, or retained by Founder)
- Cross-reference to governing document (IP Assignment Agreement, IP License Agreement, or other)

Each Founder completes their section within 30 days of executing the Agreement.

**Why This Matters:** Investor due diligence always includes an IP chain-of-title review. The diligence team will want to see: (1) what IP exists, (2) who created it, (3) who owns it now, and (4) what agreement governs the transfer. The current structure (founder → PTL via IP Assignment, PTL → TMS via IP License) is sound but not documented in a single, scannable location. Exhibit B creates that single source of truth.

## 22. Action Items / Conditions Precedent

**Current State:** Several provisions in the agreement require follow-up actions (proxy designations, IP schedule, equity incentive plan adoption, 409A valuation), but there's no consolidated list or deadline.

**Proposed Change:** Add Section 15.8 — Action Items / Conditions Precedent.

**How It Works:**

The following actions must be completed within 30 days of execution of the Agreement:

| # | Action Item | Responsible Party | Reference |
|---|------------|-------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Complete Incapacity Proxy Designation (Exhibit A) | Each Founder | Section 1.6(d) |
| 2 | Complete Pre-Existing IP Schedule (Exhibit B) | Each Founder | Section 14.2 |
| 3 | Board adoption of the Equity Incentive Plan | Board (both Founders) | Recital E |
| 4 | Engage independent appraiser for 409A valuation | Board (both Founders) | Equity Incentive Plan |
| 5 | Execute individual Stock Purchase Agreements | Each Founder | Section 2.1 |
| 6 | Open Company bank account with dual-authorization | Both Founders | Section 12.4 |

Failure to complete these items does not invalidate the Agreement, but each Founder agrees to use best efforts to complete them within the specified timeframe.

**Why This Matters:** Legal agreements often create obligations that slip through the cracks because no one tracks them. This section consolidates every outstanding to-do in one place with a clear deadline. It also aligns with the existing `TODO.md` in the formation directory, ensuring the corporate formation process moves forward in an organized way.
