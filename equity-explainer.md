# Your Equity at The Move Social

Welcome to the team. You are getting stock options — a real ownership stake in this company. This document explains what that means, how it works, and the specific protections we have built in so you do not get screwed.

---

## What You Are Getting

When you join, you receive a **stock option grant** — the right to buy shares of The Move Social at a fixed price (the "strike price"). If the company becomes worth more than your strike price, the difference is yours.

Your options are **Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs)** for **Class B Common Stock**. Class B has no voting rights, but has **identical economic rights** to Class A stock on a per-share basis. Same dividends, same distributions, same payout in an acquisition. The only difference is you do not vote on corporate matters.

*Reference: Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 1; Equity Incentive Plan, Section 3.1*

---

## Vesting

Your options vest over **four years** with a **one-year cliff**:

| Milestone | What Happens |
|-----------|-------------|
| Day 1 | Options granted, none exercisable yet (unless you early exercise) |
| 12 months | 25% of your options vest (the "cliff") |
| Months 13-48 | Remaining 75% vest monthly in equal installments |
| 48 months | 100% vested |

If you leave before the one-year cliff, you get nothing. After the cliff, you keep whatever has vested.

*Reference: Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 2; Equity Incentive Plan, Section 5.4*

## Early Exercise and Tax Optimization

Your options include an **early-exercise provision** — you can exercise your options immediately, even before they vest. Here is why this matters:

1. **You exercise immediately** at the current strike price (pennies per share)
2. **You file an 83(b) election** with the IRS within 30 days of exercise
3. **This locks in your tax basis** at today's near-zero value
4. **All future appreciation** is taxed at long-term capital gains rates (if you hold for 1+ year from exercise)

Without the 83(b) election, you would owe ordinary income tax on each vesting tranche as the company's value increases. The 83(b) election eliminates this risk entirely.

**The company will provide:**
- A pre-filled 83(b) election form in your exercise package
- A 20-day calendar reminder before the filing deadline
- Written instructions for filing via certified mail

The 30-day filing deadline is absolute. There are no extensions. We build this into the process so it cannot be missed.

**Important:** Unvested exercised shares are subject to a company repurchase right at original cost if you leave before vesting. You do not lose money — you get back what you paid.

*Reference: Stock Option Grant Agreement, Sections 3.4-3.6; Equity Incentive Plan, Section 5.6A*

## What Happens If You Leave

If you have early exercised (recommended):

| Scenario | Vested Shares | Unvested Shares |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| You leave or are let go without cause | You keep them | Company repurchases at original cost |
| Terminated for cause | Forfeited immediately | Forfeited immediately |
| Death or disability | Estate keeps vested shares | Company repurchases at original cost |

If you have **not** early exercised:

| Scenario | Vested Options | Unvested Options |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| You leave or are let go without cause | 90 days to exercise | Forfeited |
| Terminated for cause | Forfeited immediately | Forfeited immediately |
| Death or disability | 12 months to exercise | Forfeited |

"Exercise" means you pay the strike price and receive actual shares. If you do not exercise within the window, the options expire.

"Cause" is narrowly defined: felony conviction, fraud against the company, willful breach of agreements after 30 days' written notice, willful failure to perform duties after 30 days' written notice, or willful misconduct materially injuring the company. A disagreement, a bad quarter, or a personality conflict is **not** cause.

*Reference: Stock Option Grant Agreement, Sections 3.5, 4; Equity Incentive Plan, Section 5.7*

## The Problem We Are Solving

At many startups, team members hold stock options that look valuable on paper but pay out nothing in an acquisition. Here is how that typically happens:

1. Investors put in $10M and get **liquidation preferences** — they get their money back first
2. Company sells for $12M
3. Investors take $10M off the top
4. Only $2M left for everyone else — founders, team members, option holders
5. If the sale price is below $10M, option holders get **$0**

We have written specific protections into our equity documents so this does not happen to you.

---

## Protection 1: Floor Payout

If the company is acquired, you receive a **guaranteed minimum cash payment** at closing: the difference between the acquisition price per share and your strike price, times your vested shares.

This payment happens **before liquidation preferences**. It cannot be reduced or eliminated by investor priority payments. If the acquirer will not fund it, the company must reserve enough from the deal proceeds to pay you before distributing anything to preferred stockholders.

**What this means:** You get paid. Period. Investor preferences do not eat your payout.

*Reference: Equity Incentive Plan, Section 8.3; Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 6.1*

---

## Protection 2: Equal Treatment Per Share

Your Class B shares receive the **exact same per-share payout** as Class A shares (held by the founders) in any acquisition. No class of stock gets a better deal per share than yours.

The company has committed to never entering into any deal where Class B holders receive less per share than Class A holders.

**What this means:** Your shares are not second-class. A share is a share.

*Reference: Equity Incentive Plan, Section 8.4; Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 6.2*

---

## Protection 3: Automatic Vesting on Acquisition

If the company is acquired and the board does not make a decision about your options within 10 days before closing, **all your unvested options automatically vest in full**.

This prevents a scenario where an acquisition closes and your unvested options just disappear.

**What this means:** If there is an exit, you are not left holding unvested options that evaporate.

*Reference: Equity Incentive Plan, Section 8.2(d); Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 6.3*

---

## Protection 4: Investor Negotiation Covenant

The company has committed that in any future fundraising round, it will negotiate for:

- **Non-participating preferred stock** — investors choose their money back OR their pro-rata share, not both
- **Carve-outs for option holders** — a portion of any deal proceeds reserved for you before liquidation preferences apply
- **Caps on liquidation preferences** — preventing preferences from growing so large they wipe out common stockholders

This is a covenant, not a guarantee — investor negotiations involve two sides. But the commitment is in writing.

**What this means:** We have promised to fight for your interests at the negotiating table, not just our own.

*Reference: Equity Incentive Plan, Section 8.5*

---

## Protection 5: Defined "Cause"

You can only be terminated "for cause" — which triggers immediate forfeiture of all options — under a narrow, specific definition:

- Conviction of a felony or crime involving moral turpitude
- Fraud, embezzlement, or misappropriation against the company
- Willful and material breach of your written agreements
- Willful failure to perform duties for 30 days after written notice
- Willful misconduct materially injuring the company

A bad quarter, a disagreement, or a personality conflict is **not** cause. This protects you from having your options stripped on a pretext.

*Reference: Equity Incentive Plan, Section 5.8; Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 4.5*

## What Your Options Are NOT

Let us be direct about limitations:

- **Options are not cash.** They have value only if the company's value exceeds your strike price.
- **Options are not transferable.** You cannot sell them, pledge them, or give them away (except through your will).
- **Options are not voting rights.** Class B stock does not vote on corporate matters.
- **Options expire.** If you do not exercise within your window (90 days after leaving, or 10 years from grant — whichever comes first), they are gone. (If you early exercise, this does not apply — you already own the shares.)
- **There is no public market.** You cannot sell your shares on a stock exchange. Liquidity comes from an acquisition, IPO, or secondary sale — none of which are guaranteed.

*Reference: Equity Incentive Plan, Section 7; Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 5*

## Tax Basics

**NSOs with early exercise + 83(b):** When you exercise immediately and file an 83(b) election, the taxable spread (fair market value minus strike price) is near zero. You pay negligible ordinary income tax at exercise. All future appreciation is taxed as capital gain upon sale, with long-term rates applying if you hold for 1+ year from exercise.

**NSOs without 83(b):** You owe ordinary income tax on the spread at each vesting tranche as the company's value increases. This is why early exercise + 83(b) is strongly recommended.

**This is not tax advice.** Talk to a tax advisor before exercising options. The company will withhold taxes as required by law.

*Reference: Stock Option Grant Agreement, Section 7*
